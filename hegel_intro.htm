Hegel’s Aesthetics and the End of Art

Introduction: Of Old Hegels and New

Ottilie von Goethe, daughter-in-law of the famous poet and polymath, tells the following story of an encounter between her sister and the philosopher G.W.F. Hegel in the summer of 1827:

One day Goethe announced... that there would be a guest for lunch without—as was always his custom—revealing his name or introducing him as he made his appearance.... During the meal Goethe was comparatively quiet—no doubt so as not to disturb the flowing speech of his very voluble and logically penetrating guest. An entirely novel terminology, a mode of expression mentally over-leaping itself, the peculiarly employed philosophical formulations of this ever more animated man in the course of his demonstrations—all this finally reduced Goethe to silence.... The lady of the house likewise listened in silence—no doubt somewhat taken aback.... After the meal had ended and the guest departed, Goethe asked... “Now how did you like the man?”  “Strange,” she replied, “I cannot tell whether he is brilliant or mad....” “Well, well, we just dined with the most famous of modern philosophers—Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel!” (Letters, p. 711)

As Frau Goethe’s account suggests, a single afternoon spent in the company of Prof. Hegel himself was hardly sufficient to come away with any genuine understanding of what he said or thought; and, as so many books which purport to be about his philosophy bear similar witness, it seems equally possible for one to emerge from many years of study not much better informed.

Hegel’s Status Today

If such a thing could be measured, it would come as no surprise to learn that the disparity between fame and comprehension is greater for Hegel than for any other philosopher from Socrates to Wittgenstein. For various reasons—most of them dubious—Hegel’s name is known far beyond the narrow confines of academic philosophy; yet it is hard to find anyone with even the most basic grasp of anything Hegel himself actually said. 

As a result, the difference between the Hegel of repute and the Hegel who emerges from his own words could not be more dramatic. As Allen Wood suggests in a recent translation of Hegel’s Philosophy of Right, this discrepancy is most likely due to the fact that

Hegel is cited much more frequently than he is read, and discussed far oftener than he is understood. Some of those who discourse on Hegel with the greatest sophistication know him only through warped, inaccurate, or bowdlerized second-hand accounts (for instance: accounts of the Hegelian dialectic as “thesis–antithesis–synthesis”). The Hegelian ideas which capture the popular imagination are often not present in Hegel at all, or have only the most tenuous and dubious connection with what Hegel actually thought or wrote. (EPR, p. xxvii)[1]

Elsewhere Wood speaks of the subtleness and complexity of Hegel’s thought—from which, he says, “his meaning can be separated only with skilled and careful surgery, [and] even then usually not without risk of mortal injury.” (EPR, p. xxvii) As we shall see, the danger to which he refers is perhaps the greatest source of error in trying to understand Hegel—that is, the attempt somehow to separate the “meaning” or “essence” of his philosophy from his systematic presentation of it (put simply: to extract the “content” from its “form”).

The contrary approach of the present study takes its cue from Hegel himself, who remarks in his introductory lecture on the philosophy of history:

Our ideas, or our intelligence generally, demands that the whole, the universal, be surveyed, that the aim of the whole be grasped before coming down to details. We want to see the individual parts in relation to the whole—for it is only in this relation that they have their preeminent meaning and worth. (LPH, p. 53)

For Hegel, perhaps more than any other philosopher, it is necessary (if daunting) to concern oneself first and foremost with the entire range of his thought before focusing on any particular aspect of it. This approach is essential since, unlike other philosophers, Hegel is unique in having essentially only one philosophy, i.e., a single philosophical system rather than a multitude of diverse and isolated “treatments”, e.g. of ethics, theology, logic, aesthetics, etc.

In this system, for example, Hegel does not cut art off from religion, nor religion from philosophy as such, and, by his own advice, we should resist the temptation to do so ourselves—lest we fall into the same trap as those commentators who attempt, in effect, to “saw off” some piece of Hegel’s system and then proceed to criticize him on a point he would admit himself is incomprehensible severed from its context and therefore, as he would say, “in abstraction”.

People often think they have done their job when they have found something which can be justly criticized; they are right, of course, in one respect, but they are wrong insofar as they fail to recognize the positive factor. To see only the bad side in everything and to overlook all the positive and valuable qualities is a sign of extreme superficiality. (LPWH, p. 66)

If we are to avoid this superficiality and not only understand Hegel, but truly engage with his thought, we can do no better than heed his own advice from the end of the Science of Logic—where he says that in a genuine intellectual confrontation, “[we] must penetrate opponent’s stronghold and meet him on his own ground; [for] no advantage is gained by attacking him somewhere else and defeating him where he is not.” (SL, p. 581)

In the case of so many books about Hegel’s philosophy (especially in English) it is not hard to see that their authors have taken what Hegel describes as the easy (and therefore inadequate) route[2]: they pick and choose from the vast range of his thought, criticizing what they take to be its negative element, all without the slightest attempt to meet Hegel “on his own ground”. “[T]o look for the substance and enduring value of things,” Hegel says in the Philosophy of History, “is not so much equity as justice” (LPWH, p. 66)—an admonition we may adopt as the motto of our project here.

 

Obstacles

In Hegelianism: The Path toward Dialectical Humanism, a study of Hegel’s influence on the philosophical world (and beyond), John Toews quotes the eulogies of two Hegelian disciples, Friedrich Förster and Philipp Marheineke, who, in praising their departed mentor, unwittingly predict the root causes of his decline and fall from philosophical preeminence:

“Let the dead bury the dead,” Förster proclaimed at Hegel’s grave-side; “to us belongs the living; he who having thrown off his earthly chains celebrates his transfiguration.” Marheineke also placed the burial of Hegel’s earthly remains in the context of the immortal life of his spirit which [he said] “still lives and will live forever in his writings and his countless admirers and students.” In fact, Marheineke claimed that the departure of the empirical, individual Hegel allowed his eternal spirit to be appropriated by his followers in “a purer fashion than before”. “Emancipated from all sensuous appearance,” [he continued,] Hegel was now “no longer susceptible to misinterpretation,” but [instead] “transfigured in the hearts and spirits of all who recognized his eternal worth.” (Toews, H, p. 88-9)

We should not doubt that the “transfiguration” which Marheineke predicted has taken place, though clearly his opinion concerning the insusceptibility of Hegel’s thought to misinterpretation has proved false. It is, in fact, Hegel’s “transfiguration” that is his philosophy’s appeal to so many different individuals and schools of thought, which, far from guaranteeing a lasting, coherent, and unified Hegel, has resulted in a widely divergent multiplicity of Hegels (or better, “pseudo-Hegels”)—most of which, unfortunately, have little, if anything to do with the Hegel who emerges from the serious consideration of his writings and lectures taken on their own terms. It is these divergent and conflicting Hegels which represent the greatest obstacle to achieving a reasonable understanding of Hegel’s philosophy itself and its genuine value to us today and our philosophical concerns.[3]

The origin of today’s motley band of Hegels can be traced back as far as the publication of Hegel’s first book, the Phenomenology of Mind, in 1807. In the Preface to this work, Hegel asserts his conviction that “it is the nature of truth to prevail when its time has come, and that it appears only when this time has come, and therefore never... prematurely, nor finds a public not ready to receive it” (PM, ¶71). There can be little doubt, however, that far more people were bewildered than enlightened by the Phenomenology, and, further, that the unfamiliarity and difficulty of its contents were responsible for the wide range of interpretations of Hegel’s philosophy beginning with its public debut.

With Hegel’s death twenty-four years later, as suggested above, the philosophical world’s Hegel population was able to multiply unchecked (i.e. “emancipated” from the living proximity of the “empirical Hegel” himself). Thus it is no wonder—considering the vastness and intricacy of his published books and lectures—that both conservatives and revolutionaries as well as atheists and true believers alike have all acknowledged themselves as his heirs through the glorification of various aspects of his thought in isolation.

In addition to the multiplicity of interpretations of Hegel, another point to note, particularly in relation to the English-speaking tradition, concerns the unlikely appearance in 1857 of Hegel’s Lectures on the Philosophy of World History as the first of his works to be translated into English. In a recent edition of the first of these lectures, Duncan Forbes notes that Hegel’s philosophy of history is often used as a “popular introduction” to his philosophy as a whole, and that for this reason “one suspects that much of the misunderstanding and misrepresentation of Hegel has been due to this [fact].” (LPWH [Nisbett trans.], p. vii) Hegel’s philosophy of history does, in fact, contain the outline of his philosophy in total, but this content is so “speculative” (to use Hegel’s term) that one would have to have mastered the Phenomenology of Mind, the Science of Logic, and probably the three-volume Encyclopedia of the Philosophical Sciences as well before being able to recognize, let alone comprehend it.

The point here is simply that many of the bizarrest notions about Hegel—that he believed the universe to be a mind, that the natural world is “derived” from or “posited” by thought, that humans are merely pawns of the “cunning of reason,” etc.—all these may be attributed to attempts to approach him from the parts of his philosophy which appear to be the most “accessible” or “concrete,” but which, in fact, presuppose competence in its most speculative, and therefore most complex and unfamiliar aspects.

From the publication of J.H. Stirling’s influential Secret of Hegel in 1865 through Charles Taylor’s still-popular Hegel of 1975, the old-fashioned (and unfounded) image of Hegel as grand metaphysician has persisted in English-speaking philosophy virtually undisturbed for nearly a century and a half.[4]

The “New” Hegel

In the introduction to his recent book, Hegel’s Phenomenology: The Sociality of Reason, Terry Pinkard remarks:

[A]fter the Second World War... German commentators began crafting an original, precise, and insightful interpretation of the Hegelian texts that can fairly be said to have demolished (one hopes forever) most of the old myths about Hegel. In the period from roughly 1979 to the present the seeds of that German debate have begun to flower in the Anglo-American world as a whole host of new interpretations of Hegel have been emerging that have taken that German scholarship... in new directions. (Pinkard, HP, p. 3)[5]

These are significant points, indeed, but it is possible to trace the emergence of the “new” (and much improved) Hegel in Anglo-American philosophy back even further to the 1950s—especially in the work of Walter Kaufmann (who, it is interesting to note, is much better known for his commentaries and translations of Nietzsche than for his Hegel scholarship). In his Hegel: Reinterpretation, Texts, and Commentary, Kaufmann acknowledges that many years of unfavorable concretion have taken their toll on Hegel’s philosophy and he argues for the need to go back to the beginning and try, as far as possible, to “start over”. Indeed, he does so himself by presenting what he describes as an “intellectual biography” which investigates the origins of Hegel’s ideas and charts the course of their development and maturity.[6]

The intention of the present study, however, is not to return to basics with Hegel, at least not biographically speaking, but instead merely to acknowledge the innovative example of a few ground-breaking scholars and recognize them as a model for an open-minded and fair assessment of Hegel’s philosophy, particularly, in the present case, his aesthetics. As we saw Hegel declare above, this approach represents “not so much equity as justice.”

The main point to grasp here, then, is that before we try to say something new about Hegel—that is, engage critically and constructively with his philosophy—it is imperative that we know what he himself said and understand what he meant. As a first step toward familiarizing ourselves with Hegel in this way, we should briefly introduce the two most important aspects of his philosophy: one to which we have alluded already, which is “system”, and the other which is “spirit”—terms which will be significant in the chapters ahead.

Introducing “Geist”

Throughout his writings (and even in several of his book and lecture titles) Hegel employs the word “Geist”—which may be translated as both “mind” and “spirit”. Wielding Ockham’s razor, one might feel reluctant to use “spirit” in addition to “mind” since this could be interpreted as deriving the existence of two entities in English from a single German origin. Most people are wary enough of such Hegelian creatures as “the Concept,” “Absolute Idea,” “Objective Mind,” and so on, so one may legitimately wonder: Why add “Spirit” unnecessarily to this cast of characters? Further consideration, however, reveals that there are certain times when Hegel is clearly discussing far more than is ordinarily suggested by the usage of “mind” alone. I have thus retained the option to employ either “mind” or “spirit” for “Geist” in accordance with this principle. When “spirit” does appear, then, it is intended to suggest that Hegel’s subject is something greater than “mind”. What Hegel definitely should not be taken to mean, however, is some thing other than mind. “Spirit” always has to do with the mind, but it is clear that we must never construe it as also being something in addition to mind. Hegel insists upon this point often enough that we should take him at his word and refrain from making “spirit” into the autonomous entity it is often purported to be.

To initiate a discussion of the distinctions within Geist—i.e. between “spirit” and “mind”—we should consider the following points: first, it bears repeating that Hegel’s “spirit” is not a spirit, as in “holy spirit.” Instead, Geist is more akin to the broader implications of something like “Christmas spirit”. Hegel’s own particular meanings of “spirit” (and several are distinguishable) include: the spirit of the age, national spirit, world-spirit, and so on. In the Philosophy of History, for example, Hegel remarks: “spiritual totality constitutes a single being—the spirit of a nation.” (LPWH, p. 103) What he means is that there is something which may be identified as national spirit. (In the passage just quoted, Hegel is referring to the “English spirit”.) Hegel, no more than anyone else, would want to go so far as to claim that there is such a thing as the “English Spirit” existing somewhere independently of the English nation or people. The same, of course, is true of his references to “the spirit of mediaeval Europe,” the “oriental spirit,” just as it is of the most comprehensive of Hegelian spirits: the “world-spirit”—by which Hegel means the collection of national and temporal spirits considered as a whole. This “universal spirit”, he says, “is essentially present as human consciousness. Knowledge attains existence and being for itself in man. Spirit knows itself and exists for itself as a subject, and… as such, it is equivalent to human consciousness.” (LPWH, p. 95)

Scope of the Present Study

If we may say that the subject of Hegel’s philosophy is spirit, we may describe its form as a system. In Chapter One, “A Circle of Circles”, we shall properly consider the systematicity of Hegel’s philosophy, but it is necessary here to make a few introductory remarks regarding system in general and its role in determining the scope of the present study.

As noted above, Hegel’s body of philosophical work is not simply an amalgam of books and lectures treating various subjects in a philosophical manner; instead—being a system—it constitutes a unified conceptual whole composed of separate yet related elements. Keeping in mind what has been said so far about method and approach—especially Hegel’s own statements in this regard—if we are to do our job well and consider Hegel’s philosophy fairly and on its own terms, we must necessarily consider it in its entirety; for, as we shall see, any attempt to treat some particular aspect of it—in the present case: aesthetics—in isolation from the systematic philosophical whole of which it is a part is doomed to failure.

Therefore, as has already been suggested, Chapter One shall be concerned mainly with the presentation and analysis of the systematic structure and significance of Hegel’s philosophy as a whole, i.e. the groundwork upon which the major ideas of this book will be based. The material of this chapter will be drawn from the following texts: the Phenomenology of Mind, which lays the foundation for Hegel’s system, the Science of Logic which demonstrates its logical basis and structure, and the philosophical Encyclopedia which presents the Hegelian system in outline.[7]

Chapter Two, “Beauty Born of the Spirit”, presents an overview of Hegel’s aesthetics and discusses art in general and its relation to truth, freedom, nature, religion and so on.

Chapter Three, “A World of Actualized Beauty”, continues the story from the point of view of Hegel’s historical division of aesthetics into “art forms”—of which he identifies three: “symbolism”, “classicism”, and “romanticism”. Following this review of the “practical” side of Hegel’s aesthetics, Chapter Four, “Bowing the Knee No Longer ” will focus on the final—“romantic”—form of art and scrutinize the details of the “end of art” associated with it.

Chapter Five, “Philosophy Paints its Grey in Grey”, considers Hegel’s final words on the philosophy of art and art history, and discusses the possibilities of a constructive engagement with his ideas and, further, a critical development of them in light of post-Hegelian art and aesthetics.

In the concluding chapter, “The Aesthetic Dimension”, it is hoped that the so-called “problem of ‘the end of art’” will be laid to rest—allowing us to consider how, freed of this mythical burden, space may be seen to be opened up in Hegel’s aesthetics for a rich and continuing history—both artistically and philosophically.

In an Epilogue, “Obsessed with Originality,” we part company with Hegel in order to consider the example of contemporary Slovene artist Mojca Oblak’s “Transcendental Mannerism” and how it may be seen to constitute a genuine contribution to the post-Hegelian history of art and philosophy, and even human experience and cognition themselves.

Concluding Introductory Remarks

It may perhaps be true to say that ultimately this is not a book “about” Hegel nor one which attempts to negotiate the body of Hegel scholarship and commentary which has expanded rapidly in recent years, especially in the English-speaking philosophical world.  Instead, this is a study which makes use of Hegel’s ideas—looking into them and into his way of thinking in order to draw out their implications and expand upon them. It does not, however, end up saying the same thing as Hegel; nor does it refute him. We shall consider the same subject Hegel considers in his aesthetics, and, in large part, we shall consider it in the same way. Where we differ, however, is in our conclusions—not because we will conclude something altogether different from Hegel... just slightly more.  Contrary to received opinion, Hegel’s is a philosophy deeply rooted in history and closely tied to real phenomena; and contrary also to his own references to his philosophy as “speculative reason”, Hegel himself was no speculator in the modern sense. Hegel was not interested (philosophically) in the future, nor will the future concern us here. It is, of course, true that much that was future for Hegel is now past for us; and much of what we will be considering in the more “constructive” part of this book Hegel neither knew nor speculated upon, but now constitutes the territory of our own reflection. Much has changed in the world and its cultural productions since Hegel’s time, but his philosophy, as such, is not indifferent to these products, and indeed, as we shall see, it continues to provide valuable insights into them. This, then, is our project: not simply to try to guess what Hegel might have said about Cubism or postmodern architecture, but to determine what they and other artistic phenomena mean in the light of his philosophy, and what—philosophically speaking—art itself is capable of achieving in response to what Hegel calls “the needs of spirit”. It is my contention that we still have much to learn, and my hope is that this book may be considered a contribution to this continuing project.
