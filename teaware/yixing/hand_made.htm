In China a teapot can cost a few dollars in a market (above) or tens of thousands of dollars at auction. Disregarding antique pots and those by famous contemporary masters, the difference in the market today essentially comes down to the quality of workmanship and material.
While most people probably think all Yixing teapots are hand-made, it turns out, in fact, that most made in molds. To compare: a worker in a factory can produce up to 100 teapots a day, while, in the same time, an artisan using traditional methods can make only a few (which will require several additional days of work for finishing).
Mass produced teapots have only two parts (lid & body) which are molded, while an artist's pot typically has five (lid, body, bottom, spout and handle) which are individually formed by hand and assembled into a finished pot.
THINGS TO LOOK FOR: Handmade Yixing teapots are not molded or made on a wheel. The body is most often made from a single piece of clay which has been pounded flat to an even thickness. Inside, you should be able to see and/or feel where the two ends were joined together and also where the handle was attached to the body. It should also be apparent (from tooling inside and/or a visible seam on the outside) that the body and bottom were made separately and joined together by hand.
