In China a teapot can cost a few dollars in a market stall (above) or tens of thousands of dollars at auction. Disregarding antique pots and those by famous contemporary masters, the difference in the market today essentially comes down to the quality of workmanship and material.

While most people assume Yixing teapots are handmade, the truth is that most most are made in molds. To compare: a worker in a factory can produce up to 100 teapots a day, while, in the same time, an artisan using traditional methods can make only a few (which can require several additional days for finishing).

Mass-produced molded pots have only two parts (lid & body), while an artist's teapot typically has five (lid, body, bottom, spout and handle) which are individually handmade and assembled into a finished pot.

THINGS TO LOOK FOR: Handmade Yixing teapots are not molded or made on a wheel. The body is most often made from a single piece of clay which has been pounded flat to an even thickness. Inside, you should be able to see and/or feel where the two ends were joined together and also where the handle was attached to the body. It should also be apparent (from tooling inside and/or a visible seam on the outside) that the body and bottom were made separately and joined together by hand (rather than molded from a single piece of clay).

Even on handmade pots, the lid is not always entirely handmade. Sometimes it is formed with the assistance of a template to achieve a better match with the body. Making a lid without a template is more difficult, which makes an all-handmade pot more valuable. To identify a handmade lid, look at the cylindrical part that nestles in the pot for the seam where it was joined to the upper part of the lid and feel for slight inconsistencies of thickness, suggesting it was formed by hand rather than molded.
